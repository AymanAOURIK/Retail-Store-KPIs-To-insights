# Executive Summary

This exploratory data analysis reviewed weekly retail KPI performance across 10 stores for 2024 and 2025, using traffic, transactions, quantity sold, and net sales as the core business measures. The dataset was structurally strong overall: there were no missing values, no duplicate store-week records, and the schema was consistent with the expected analytical grain of one row per store per week. The main data-quality limitation is that 2025 contains only weeks 1 to 48, while 2024 contains weeks 1 to 52. As a result, year-over-year comparisons must be made on a like-for-like basis using weeks 1 to 48 only. One additional exception was identified in `Store_G` for week 21 of 2025, where transactions exceeded traffic, which should be reviewed as a likely data anomaly.

The analysis was organized around a sales driver framework: net sales were decomposed into traffic, conversion rate, units per transaction, and average selling price. This allowed performance differences to be explained in operational terms rather than through raw rankings alone. At the network level, the most important conclusion is that the business appears to be facing a traffic challenge more than an in-store execution challenge. When comparing 2025 to 2024 on a comparable week range, average net sales declined by 5.4%, while traffic declined by 12.1%. At the same time, most efficiency measures improved: conversion rate, units per transaction, average transaction value, and revenue per visitor all increased on average. This suggests stores were generally better at converting and monetizing visitors, but they were doing so with a smaller shopper base.

Store performance also showed a clear separation between scale and efficiency. `Store_A`, `Store_B`, and `Store_D` generated the highest total net sales and together accounted for 43.7% of total revenue, making them the main contributors to network performance. However, the highest-volume stores were not always the most productive on a per-visitor basis. `Store_F` stood out for exceptionally high traffic but weaker efficiency, indicating a volume-led model. In contrast, `Store_E` and `Store_G` stood out for stronger commercial efficiency, especially on conversion and revenue per visitor, despite operating with lower traffic. This pattern suggests that stores are not underperforming for the same reasons: some are constrained by demand generation, while others are constrained by conversion, basket size, or pricing power.

Year-over-year performance was mixed across the estate. Only four of the ten stores grew net sales when comparing weeks 1 to 48 of 2025 against the same period in 2024. `Store_C` and `Store_F` were the strongest growth cases, but for different reasons: `Store_C` improved primarily through stronger basket economics and price realization, while `Store_F` grew mainly through higher traffic. `Store_A` and `Store_D` were broadly stable because stronger conversion and basket dynamics offset weaker traffic or pricing pressure. On the other side, `Store_H` was the weakest-performing store, showing deterioration across multiple drivers including traffic, basket size, pricing, and revenue productivity. This makes `Store_H` the clearest priority for deeper operational review.

The weekly analysis also revealed meaningful seasonality and volatility. Sales peaks were concentrated around weeks 51, 26, 48, 2, and 27, while weaker periods appeared around weeks 8, 33, 32, 11, and 6. This suggests that calendar timing, promotions, or local commercial events materially influence performance and should be incorporated into future planning and target-setting. Anomaly detection performed within each store-year context flagged 257 unusual KPI observations, mostly in traffic and transactions. These outliers should not automatically be treated as errors, but they do mark weeks that warrant business follow-up to determine whether they reflect promotions, closures, operational disruption, or data issues.

Overall, the EDA points to three strategic implications. First, restoring traffic is likely the biggest lever for improving total sales performance at the network level. Second, stores should be managed by archetype rather than with a single performance lens, because high-volume stores and high-efficiency stores face different constraints. Third, the combination of seasonal swings and flagged anomaly weeks suggests that weekly performance management should be tied more closely to promotional calendars, store operations, and local context. The next phase of analysis should focus on explaining traffic declines, investigating the drivers behind outlier weeks, and prioritizing intervention for stores with broad-based deterioration, especially `Store_H`.
